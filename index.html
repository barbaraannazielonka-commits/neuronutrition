Neuroscience

How food rewires
your brain

This isn't about being healthy in a vague sense. These are specific, measurable biological mechanisms happening inside your skull right now.

🧠

Synaptogenesis & Pruning

During adolescence, your brain creates millions of new synaptic connections, then prunes the unused ones, in a "use it or lose it" system. Omega-3 fatty acids (DHA especially) are the structural material synapses are built from. Without them, this process is compromised.

Structural Development
⚡

Myelination

Myelin is the fatty insulation wrapped around nerve fibres, which is what makes signals travel faster. The prefrontal cortex (your decision-making, impulse-control centre) is still myelinating through your mid-20s. Saturated fats and trans fats from ultra-processed food can disrupt this process.

Neural Speed
💬

Neurotransmitter Production

Serotonin (mood), dopamine (motivation), and GABA (calm focus) are built from amino acids you get from food. Tryptophan → serotonin. Tyrosine → dopamine. Without protein diversity and key micronutrients, your brain literally cannot manufacture the chemicals that regulate how you feel and think.

Mood & Motivation
🔥

Neuroinflammation

Ultra-processed diets trigger low-grade systemic inflammation that crosses the blood-brain barrier. This neuroinflammation directly impairs synaptogenesis and has been linked to smaller hippocampal volume, the brain region central to learning and memory.

Brain Protection
🦠

The Gut-Brain Axis

Your gut contains 500 million neurons and produces ~90% of your body's serotonin. Diet shapes your gut microbiome composition, which in turn influences mood, anxiety levels, and cognitive clarity through the vagus nerve, a direct neural highway to your brain.

Gut-Brain Connection
🌱

BDNF: Brain Fertiliser

Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF) is a protein that supports neuron survival and growth. It's essentially fertiliser for your brain. Diet directly modulates BDNF levels. Omega-3s and polyphenols increase it, while sugar and saturated fats suppress it.

Neuroplasticity
Brain Development Timeline

Why your teen years
are critical

The brain does not fully mature until approximately age 25. Adolescence is the second great critical period of development. the first being the first 3 years of life. Nutritional choices during this window have a disproportionate, long-lasting effect.

Ages 12 to 14

Amygdala Growth

Emotional regulation centres expand rapidly. Iron and zinc are critical: deficiencies alter dopamine and serotonin pathways, affecting mood and behaviour.

Ages 14 to 17

Peak Synaptic Density

Maximum synapse formation. DHA from omega-3s is the literal building block. High-sugar diets actively suppress BDNF during this peak window.

Ages 17 to 21

Prefrontal Cortex Myelination

Decision-making, impulse control, and long-term planning circuits are being insulated. The quality of dietary fats consumed has a direct structural impact.

Ages 21 to 25

Final Pruning & Consolidation

The brain finalises its architecture. Antioxidants and anti-inflammatory nutrients protect gains made during earlier phases of development.

What to Watch Out For

Norwegian teens' biggest
dietary threats

These aren't just "unhealthy" in a generic sense. Here's exactly what they do to your developing brain, mechanism by mechanism.

⚠ High Risk

⚡ Energy Drinks

Red Bull, Monster, Celsius & similar
  • Up to 80mg caffeine per can disrupts adenosine receptors, collapsing deep-sleep architecture, the phase when memory consolidation and neural repair occur
  • High sugar load (27g+ per 250ml) triggers dopamine spikes that desensitise reward receptors over time
  • Taurine + caffeine combination amplifies cortisol, keeping the nervous system in a low-grade stress state that impairs learning
  • Regular use associated with anxiety, palpitations, and disrupted circadian rhythms in adolescents
⚠ High Risk

🍕 Ultra-Processed Food

Fast food, instant noodles, packaged snacks
  • NOVA Group 4 foods contain emulsifiers that compromise the gut lining, promoting systemic inflammation that crosses into the brain
  • Trans fats and refined seed oils compete with omega-3s for incorporation into neuronal membranes, reducing membrane fluidity
  • Studies link high UPF consumption to measurably smaller hippocampal volume in imaging studies
  • Depletes B vitamins (B12, folate, B6) critical for neurotransmitter synthesis and myelin maintenance
⚠ Moderate Risk

🍬 High-Sugar Diets

Soda, candy, sweetened yoghurt, juice
  • Chronic high sugar suppresses BDNF production, reducing the brain's capacity for learning and memory formation
  • Blood glucose spikes and crashes create "brain fog" periods that impair concentration during school hours
  • Alters gut microbiome composition, reducing production of mood-stabilising short-chain fatty acids
  • Drives neuroinflammation through advanced glycation end-products (AGEs) that damage neural tissue
⚠ Moderate Risk

☀️ Skipping Breakfast

Common pattern among Norwegian students
  • The brain uses ~20% of the body's glucose. Fasting through morning classes means your prefrontal cortex runs on empty
  • Breakfast skipping is associated with lower working memory scores and reduced attention span in controlled studies
  • Triggers cortisol surge in the morning, priming the brain for stress reactivity rather than learning mode
  • Missing key morning protein means delayed amino acid availability for neurotransmitter production throughout the day
What to Eat Instead

Foods that genuinely
boost brain performance

Each of these has specific, researched mechanisms, not just vague health claims.

🐟

Fatty Fish

Salmon, mackerel, and sardines are the richest sources of DHA, the omega-3 that forms neuronal membranes and is directly linked to BDNF production.

DHA / EPA
🫐

Blueberries

Anthocyanins cross the blood-brain barrier and protect hippocampal neurons from oxidative stress. Studies show improved memory after regular consumption.

Anthocyanins
🥚

Eggs

Rich in choline, the precursor to acetylcholine, the neurotransmitter responsible for memory and muscle control. Also contains all essential amino acids.

Choline / B12
🌰

Walnuts

Among the highest plant sources of omega-3 ALA. Also contain polyphenols that reduce neuroinflammation and support hippocampal function.

ALA / Polyphenols
🥦

Broccoli & Dark Greens

Packed with vitamin K, folate, and glucosinolates. Folate is essential for DNA methylation and neurotransmitter synthesis. Vitamin K supports sphingolipid metabolism in the brain.

Folate / Vit K
🎃

Pumpkin Seeds

Excellent source of zinc, magnesium, iron, and copper, four micronutrients that have critical roles in nerve signalling and neurotransmitter function.

Zinc / Magnesium
🍫

Dark Chocolate (70%+)

Flavonoids in cocoa increase cerebral blood flow and BDNF levels. Theobromine provides a gentler, longer-lasting focus boost than caffeine without the crash.

Flavonoids / Theobromine
🫒

Olive Oil

Oleocanthal in extra-virgin olive oil has anti-inflammatory properties similar to ibuprofen, protecting neurons from the kind of low-grade inflammation that impairs cognition.

Oleocanthal / Polyphenols
🫘

Lentils & Legumes

Slow-digesting carbohydrates provide stable blood glucose, crucial for sustained cognitive performance without the crash. Also a key plant source of iron and B vitamins.

Iron / B Vitamins
🍵

Green Tea

Contains L-theanine, an amino acid that increases alpha brain waves, associated with calm, focused alertness. Synergises with caffeine to improve attention without jitteriness.

Vocabulary

Key neuroscience terms
explained

Understanding the vocabulary helps you think more clearly about the science, and sounds impressive in biology class.

Synaptogenesis
/ si-nap-toh-JEN-uh-sis /
The formation of new synapses, the connections between neurons. This process is extremely active during adolescence and depends on omega-3 fatty acids (especially DHA) as structural material.
Example: "Eating salmon regularly supports synaptogenesis during the teenage years."
Synaptic Pruning
/ SIN-ap-tic PROO-ning /
The brain's process of eliminating unused synaptic connections, the classic use it or lose it principle. An essential part of adolescent brain development that makes frequently-used neural pathways stronger and more efficient.
Example: "Skills you practise regularly are protected from synaptic pruning."
Myelination
/ MY-uh-lin-AY-shun /
The process of wrapping nerve fibres in myelin, a fatty insulating sheath that dramatically speeds up electrical signal transmission. The prefrontal cortex continues myelinating into the mid-20s.
Example: "Disrupted myelination in the prefrontal cortex can impair impulse control."
Prefrontal Cortex (PFC)
/ pre-FRUN-tal KOR-teks /
The front region of the brain responsible for planning, decision-making, impulse control, and moderating social behaviour. The last brain region to fully mature, making it especially vulnerable during adolescence.
Example: "Poor impulse control in teens is partly because the prefrontal cortex is still developing."
Hippocampus
/ hip-oh-KAM-pus /
A seahorse-shaped structure deep in the brain critical for forming new memories, spatial navigation, and emotional regulation. Studies show its volume is measurably smaller in people who consistently eat ultra-processed diets.
Example: "The hippocampus converts short-term experiences into long-term memories during sleep."
Neurotransmitter
/ nyoo-roh-TRANS-mit-er /
Chemical messengers that transmit signals between neurons across synapses. Key examples: serotonin (mood), dopamine (reward/motivation), GABA (calm focus), acetylcholine (memory). All are synthesised from dietary amino acids and nutrients.
Example: "Low tryptophan intake can reduce serotonin production, affecting mood."
Neuroinflammation
/ nyoo-roh-in-flam-AY-shun /
Inflammation occurring within the brain and nervous system. Triggered by poor diet, it can impair synaptogenesis, reduce hippocampal volume, and is associated with depression and cognitive decline.
Example: "Ultra-processed food promotes neuroinflammation through disrupted gut barrier function."
BDNF
Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor
A protein that acts like fertiliser for neurons, supporting their survival, growth, and formation of new connections. Omega-3s and exercise increase BDNF; chronic sugar consumption suppresses it.
Example: "Regular aerobic exercise combined with a good diet significantly elevates BDNF levels."
Gut-Brain Axis
/ gut brayn AK-sis /
The bidirectional communication network between the gastrointestinal tract and the brain, operating via the vagus nerve, immune system, and neurotransmitter signalling. The gut microbiome composition profoundly influences mood and cognition.
Example: "Gut dysbiosis from poor diet can contribute to anxiety via the gut-brain axis."
Dopamine
/ DOH-pa-meen /
A neurotransmitter central to reward, motivation, and pleasure. Ultra-processed foods and energy drinks trigger dopamine spikes that can desensitise receptors over time, reducing motivation and requiring higher stimulation to feel satisfied.
Example: "Repeated sugar-induced dopamine spikes can blunt the reward response over time."
Serotonin
/ ser-oh-TOE-nin /
A neurotransmitter that regulates mood, appetite, and sleep. About 90% is produced in the gut (not the brain), making dietary choices that support gut health directly relevant to serotonin levels and emotional wellbeing.
Example: "Tryptophan from eggs and turkey is converted into serotonin in the gut lining."
Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)
/ blud brayn BAR-ee-er /
A highly selective membrane separating circulating blood from the brain's extracellular fluid. It filters out most harmful substances, but inflammatory cytokines triggered by poor diet can breach it, causing neuroinflammation.
Example: "Neuroinflammation occurs when inflammatory signals cross the blood-brain barrier."
Neuroplasticity
/ nyoo-roh-plas-TIS-i-tee /
The brain's ability to reorganise itself by forming new neural connections. Highest during adolescence and childhood. Supported by omega-3s, exercise, and BDNF, but diminished by chronic stress, sleep deprivation, and poor diet.
Example: "Learning a new language is easier for teens due to higher neuroplasticity."
DHA (Docosahexaenoic Acid)
/ dok-oh-sah-hex-ah-EE-no-ik /
An omega-3 fatty acid that makes up roughly 40% of polyunsaturated fats in the brain. Essential for neuronal membrane integrity, synaptogenesis, and BDNF production. Found primarily in oily fish and algae.
Example: "DHA from fatty fish is directly incorporated into developing neuronal membranes."
Adenosine
/ ah-DEN-oh-seen /
A molecule that builds up in the brain throughout the day and promotes sleepiness. Caffeine works by blocking adenosine receptors, delaying the sleep signal. Energy drinks' caffeine disrupts this natural rhythm, impairing sleep architecture.
Example: "Caffeine in energy drinks blocks adenosine, masking fatigue without addressing it."
Microbiome
/ my-kro-BY-ohm /
The trillions of microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, viruses) living in the gut. Diet is the primary determinant of microbiome composition and, through the gut-brain axis, the microbiome directly influences mood, stress response, and cognitive function.
Example: "A diverse, fibre-rich diet promotes a healthy microbiome and better mood regulation."

Myths and Misconceptions

10 things people
get wrong about food and the brain

These misconceptions are everywhere: in adverts, on social media, and in everyday conversation. Here is what the neuroscience actually says.

01
❌
Myth
Sugar helps you think better.
✅
Fact
Sugar may provide a quick burst of energy, but it does not improve learning or intelligence. Frequent consumption of high-sugar foods may even impair attention and memory over time.
02
❌
Myth
Energy drinks make you smarter.
✅
Fact
Energy drinks can temporarily increase alertness because of caffeine, but they do not improve learning, memory, or intelligence. They may also disrupt sleep, which is essential for brain function.
03
❌
Myth
Breakfast does not matter for your brain.
✅
Fact
A balanced breakfast can improve attention, mood, and memory for many teenagers. Meals that include protein, fibre, and complex carbohydrates help provide steady energy for learning.
04
❌
Myth
Brain supplements can replace a healthy diet.
✅
Fact
Most people get the nutrients their brains need from a varied diet. Supplements can be helpful in specific cases, such as nutrient deficiencies, but they cannot replace healthy eating habits.
05
❌
Myth
One superfood can dramatically boost your brain.
✅
Fact
There is no magic brain food. Long-term eating habits have a much greater impact on brain health than any single food.
06
❌
Myth
Fat is bad for your brain.
✅
Fact
Healthy fats are essential for brain structure and function. Omega-3 fatty acids, found in foods like fish, walnuts, and flaxseeds, play an important role in brain development and cognition.
07
❌
Myth
Coffee can replace sleep.
✅
Fact
Caffeine may help you feel more awake, but it cannot replace sleep. Sleep is crucial for memory, learning, emotional regulation, and overall brain health.
08
❌
Myth
Eating sugary foods is the best way to fuel your brain.
✅
Fact
The brain uses glucose for energy, but it gets a steady supply from nutritious foods such as whole grains, fruits, vegetables, and legumes. These provide more stable energy than sugary snacks.
09
❌
Myth
If you are young, what you eat does not affect your brain.
✅
Fact
The teenage brain is still developing. Nutrition influences learning, concentration, mood, memory, and long-term brain health.
10
❌
Myth
Healthy eating only affects your body, not your mind.
✅
Fact
Research shows that diet is linked to mood, attention, memory, learning, and mental well-being. What you eat can influence both your physical and mental health.

Veggie Omelette with Spinach and Mushrooms

A three-egg omelette filled with sautéed spinach, mushrooms, cherry tomatoes, and a little feta cheese. Served with a slice of whole grain toast and green tea.

Choline B Vitamins L-Theanine Iron
Brain benefit: Mushrooms contain ergothioneine, a unique antioxidant that accumulates in brain tissue. Green tea L-theanine promotes calm focus as you ease into the week ahead.
Lunch
🍵
Homemade Fish Soup with Root Vegetables

A warming broth with cod, salmon chunks, carrot, celeriac, leek, and fresh dill. Served with a thick slice of whole grain bread and a drizzle of olive oil.

DHA / EPA Iodine Beta-carotene Selenium
Brain benefit: Iodine from fish is critical for thyroid function, which directly regulates the pace of brain development in adolescence. This is a commonly overlooked micronutrient.
Dinner
🍗
Slow-Cooked Chicken with Lentils and Fresh Herbs

Chicken thighs slow-cooked with Puy lentils, roasted root vegetables, garlic, rosemary, and thyme. Finished with a walnut and parsley gremolata and dark bread.

Tryptophan Iron ALA B6
Brain benefit: Chicken tryptophan combined with lentil iron and B6 creates the ideal conditions for serotonin synthesis before sleep, supporting overnight memory consolidation.

🧠 Making this work in real life

🛒
Shop once, eat well all week

Salmon, eggs, lentils, oats, frozen berries, and olive oil cover most of this plan. Buy in bulk on the weekend to reduce cost and effort.

⏱️
Prep overnight oats the evening before

Monday and any overnight oat breakfast takes 3 minutes the night before. No excuses for skipping breakfast when it is already waiting in the fridge.

🫙
Knekkebrød is your best friend

Norwegian crispbread with salmon, tuna, or egg takes 4 minutes to prepare and delivers more brain-supporting nutrients per calorie than almost any other lunch.

🍫
Dark chocolate as your study snack

A square or two of 70 percent cocoa chocolate provides flavonoids that increase cerebral blood flow, giving you a real cognitive boost during revision sessions.

💧
Drink water before anything else

Start every morning with a full glass of water before eating. Even mild overnight dehydration impairs attention during first period at school.

🌿
Colour is your measure of quality

If your plate has four or more distinct colours from real food, you are almost certainly getting the polyphenol diversity your brain needs to fight neuroinflammation.
